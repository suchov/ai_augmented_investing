## Business
- **How does it earn money?** Charter sells internet, mobile, video, and voice services under the Spectrum brand across 41 states, reaching ~58 million homes/businesses. LTM revenue is $54.6B. The core engine is residential broadband; mobile (Spectrum Mobile, 12.1M lines) is the growth diversifier, while legacy video is in secular decline.
- **Moat?** Yes, but an eroding one. The moat is a **cost/infrastructure advantage** — a second-largest-U.S.-cable network built at enormous scale that would be prohibitively expensive to replicate. There are modest **switching costs** via bundling (mobile + broadband retention). Critically, the report shows this moat is being breached: 120,000 broadband subs lost in Q1 2026 (-455,000 YoY) to fixed-wireless and fiber, so the "local broadband monopoly" thesis is weakening.
- **Competitors:** T-Mobile and Verizon (fixed wireless access), fiber overbuilders, and cable peer Comcast (CMCSA). Deutsche Telekom (DTEG.F) is listed as a peer benchmark.

## Financials
- **Growth (report figures):** Revenue CAGR (5y) 2.63%; forward revenue CAGR (5y) 0.48% — essentially flat. Net income CAGR (5y) 9.13%, but net income growth was -5.03% most recently. EPS has risen (30.7 in 2022 → 37.0 LTM), driven substantially by share count shrinking from 155.2M to 123.0M via buybacks rather than by top-line growth. **FCF CAGR: not directly given**, though FCF yield jumped from 4.93% (2023) to 14.4% (2025), and levered FCF finally showed $1,449M in Q1 2026 after reporting $0 in prior periods.
- **Returns on capital:** ROE 30.2%, ROI 8.94%, ROA 3.74%. No explicit ROIC. ROE is flattered by heavy leverage, so treat it skeptically. Profitability rated 7/10; Adjusted EBITDA margins ~38–40.7%.
- **Balance sheet — the central concern:** Total debt ~$96.8B (report cites a $94–$110B range), net debt/EBITDA of ~4.15x–4.4x. Total liabilities $133.6B against total equity of $21.1B. A Pro Tip flags "short-term obligations exceed liquid assets" (current assets $4.96B vs. current liabilities $12.4B). This is a highly levered balance sheet with meaningful refinancing risk if rates stay elevated.

## Risks
- **Regulatory:** Net neutrality shifts, spectrum policy, and post–"Salt Typhoon" cybersecurity mandates could raise compliance costs and constrain monetization. The Cox deal has already cleared FCC and DOJ.
- **AI / technological disruption:** Not framed as AI, but technological substitution is the core threat — fixed wireless access (5G) and fiber are structurally undercutting cable broadband. A potential SpaceX direct-to-cell partnership is cited as both an opportunity and evidence of shifting technology.
- **Customer/supplier concentration:** Not covered in report (no customer concentration disclosed). Execution/supply-chain strain flagged around simultaneous DOCSIS 4.0 rollout, rural buildout, and Cox integration.
- **Other red flags:** Persistent broadband and video subscriber losses; ~$11B capex suppressing FCF (cash flow rated 4/10, FCF yield stated as 0.21); EPS revisions negative (4 down vs. 2 up, FY26 estimate cut 4.9%); technicals signal Sell; no dividend, so returns hinge entirely on buybacks (~$963M in Q1 2026); stock down 66.5% over one year.

## Valuation
- **Fair value:** $239.38 vs. price of $138.02 → **+73.4% implied upside**. Forward P/E just 3.1x; EV/EBITDA 5.24x; analyst targets span $124–$413 (median $229.65), signaling deep disagreement. Recent analyst ratings skew Hold/Sell (Wells Fargo Sell $160; UBS Hold $235).
- **Cheap, fair, or rich?** On a static basis it looks **statistically cheap** — a 3x P/E and 21% FCF yield are distressed-equity multiples. But the cheapness is conditional: the P/E compressed from 12.2x (2023) to 3.1x precisely because the market doubts earnings durability amid subscriber losses and a 4.4x-levered balance sheet. This is a "cheap for a reason" situation, not obvious value.
- **Bull (what market may be missing):** Capex normalizing from ~$11B to ~$7B by 2028 could inflect FCF sharply; mobile is the fastest-growing U.S. line (368K adds in Q1); Cox adds ~6.5M customers, 70M+ households, and $800M synergies; consolidation optionality (SpaceX, Comcast/NBCU spinoff dynamics).
- **Bear (what could go wrong):** Broadband erosion accelerates faster than mobile/FCF recovery; high leverage limits flexibility and amplifies any refinancing shock; simultaneous mega-projects (DOCSIS 4.0, rural, Cox) create execution risk; the FCF inflection simply arrives late or smaller than modeled.

## Verdict
Yes — a patient value investor should dig deeper, because a genuine broadband franchise trading at 3x earnings with a projected FCF inflection is exactly the kind of hated, out-of-favor asset that rewards owner-minded work. But this is a leveraged equity stub, not a fortress: at ~4.4x net debt/EBITDA with equity ($21B) a fraction of debt ($97B), the entire thesis lives or dies on free cash flow. The single most important thing to verify next is **whether the capex-driven FCF inflection is real and sustainable** — specifically, confirm that the $1.45B levered FCF in Q1 2026 is durable (not a working-capital quirk after years of reported $0), model post-Cox pro forma leverage and interest coverage, and stress-test whether broadband subscriber/ARPU trends can stabilize before debt maturities force the issue. If FCF holds and leverage is refinanceable, the discount is a gift; if not, the cheap multiple is a value trap.
