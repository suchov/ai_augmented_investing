## Business
- **How it earns money:** Subscription-based real estate data, analytics, and marketplaces. Over 95% of revenue is recurring subscription; annual contracts were 73% of Q1'26 revenue. LTM revenue $3.4B split across CoStar Suite ($813M), Multifamily/Apartments.com ($878M), Residential/Homes.com ($536M), LoopNet ($369M), Commercial RE ($331M), Info Services ($120M), plus Matterport/3D. Renewal rates run 89% overall, 95% for 5+ year customers.
- **Moat:** Yes — a deep **data moat**. ~80% share in commercial RE data, a 40-year proprietary database with 2.4 trillion data fields and 1,500+ researchers, described as "near-insurmountable." Reinforced by **switching costs** (sticky subscriptions, high renewal) and **brand** (27+ brands, 150M+ monthly visitors). The residential side (Homes.com) is not yet a moat — it's a cash-burning land grab.
- **Competitors:** Zillow (ZG) and Redfin in residential "Portal Wars"; Google's Enhanced Local Services Ads encroaching; TheRealReal (TRC) listed as a peer; Zillow also an antitrust litigation adversary over MLS data.

## Financials
- **Growth:** Revenue CAGR (5y) 14.4% (vs. Zillow 9.7%); revenue forecast CAGR (5y) ~14.1%. LTM revenue $3.4B, up ~21–23% YoY. **EPS CAGR: negative/collapsing** — diluted EPS fell from $0.93 (2022) to $0.02 (2025); Net Income CAGR (5y) is −50.1%. Analyst EPS forecasts project +56% (FY26), +32% (FY27), +25% (FY28) off a depressed base. **FCF CAGR: not meaningfully covered** — FCF yield near zero, EV/FCF negative in 2024–2025.
- **ROIC / returns on capital:** Poor. ROE 0.30%, ROI −0.20%, ROA 0.24%. Operating income was −$26M LTM (−$54M in 2025). Profitability rating 3/10. Returns are currently being destroyed by the Homes.com investment.
- **Balance sheet:** Healthy. More cash than debt; total debt ~$1.14B against $7.95B equity and $10.2B assets; liquid assets exceed short-term obligations. Book value ~$18.8/share vs. $30 price (P/B ~1.55). Notably, current assets dropped from $5.5B (2023) to $1.7B (Q1'26) as cash was deployed into acquisitions and buybacks — worth watching.

## Risks
- **Regulatory:** Material. Multiple federal antitrust class-actions alleging monopolistic practices in CRE data, plus Zillow MLS litigation. Ironic given the 80% share that is the moat — a forced data-sharing remedy would attack the core asset.
- **AI / tech disruption:** Report frames AI as tailwind (Homes AI engagement, cost efficiencies), but Google's expansion into home listings is a genuine threat to residential user-acquisition economics.
- **Concentration:** Not a single-customer risk (broad subscriber base), but heavy dependence on the U.S. CRE cycle and on the still-unproven Homes.com bet.
- **Other red flags:** Net new bookings ($67M) declined sequentially for the **third straight quarter** despite heavy sales investment — the most important yellow flag. Inexperienced Homes.com inside sales force. $550M net planned investment into Homes.com in 2026 keeping FCF near zero. Residential still −$29M adjusted EBITDA. Nasdaq-100 removal (passive selling). Activist scrutiny. Apartment construction starts down 73% from 2022 peak (15-year low), pressuring core demand. Trailing P/E of 490x.

## Valuation
- **Fair value:** ~$40.85 vs. ~$30 price → **+36.1% upside**. Analyst targets range $26 (Wells Fargo Sell) to $70 (JPMorgan), median $47.50.
- **Cheap/fair/rich:** On trailing earnings it looks absurdly rich (P/E ~490x, EV/EBITDA 44.9x) because earnings are artificially depressed by discretionary investment. On forward numbers and revenue it looks cheap: Fwd P/E ~21.9x (FY26) → 16.9x (FY27), EV/EBITDA ~15x → ~11.7x, and EV/Revenue just ~3.2x versus historical 9–15x. Verdict hinges entirely on whether the Homes.com spend converts to earnings.
- **Bull (what market may be missing):** The moat business is intact and compounding; 60 consecutive quarters of double-digit growth; residential turns EBITDA-positive Q2'26; Homes.com ARPU/monetization inflection (11x subscriber ROI, price increases, 92% run-rate growth) plus Zonda accretion and international expansion could re-rate the stock as spending normalizes. 13 of 15 recent EPS revisions were upward.
- **Bear (what could go wrong):** Bookings keep decelerating, Homes.com payback stretches out, antitrust rulings force data-sharing, CRE macro stays depressed, and the "investment" turns out to be permanent competitive spend against Zillow/Google rather than a J-curve. Then normalized earnings never justify even the compressed forward multiple.

## Verdict
Yes — a patient value investor should dig deeper. This is a genuine wide-moat, ~95%-recurring-revenue franchise (the CRE data business) trading at a decade-low valuation because a large, self-inflicted growth investment (Homes.com) is masking the earnings power of the core. That is exactly the kind of "temporarily-obscured quality" setup value investors want, and the balance sheet (net cash) buys time. But the whole thesis rests on one question, and it's the single most important thing to verify next: **is Homes.com actually gaining durable, monetizable traction, or is it a bottomless spend against Zillow/Google?** The tell is net new bookings — three consecutive sequential declines despite heavy sales investment is the report's loudest warning. I'd want to confirm bookings re-accelerate in Q2'26 (management's claimed seasonally strongest quarter) and that residential truly hits EBITDA profitability as promised, before trusting the forward-earnings ramp that makes the stock look cheap.
