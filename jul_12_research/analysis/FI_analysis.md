## Business
- **How it earns money:** Fiserv is a global payments and fintech provider. LTM revenue of $21.1B splits into Processing & Services ($16.7B) and Product ($1.1B). Core lines: merchant acquiring/point-of-sale (Clover), payment processing, digital banking, core banking (Finxact), billing, and debit network routing (STAR/Accel). It serves banks, credit unions, and merchants in 100+ countries. Reportable segments referenced are Merchant Solutions and Financial Solutions.
- **Moat:** Yes — primarily **switching costs**. The SWOT cites "dominant market share in financial technology infrastructure" with "high switching costs driven by long-term contracts and deeply integrated services." There is also a **network effect / regulatory** angle in the STAR debit network (115M+ cardholders, 2,800+ FIs), and a **scale/cost advantage** in processing. However, the moat is showing cracks: "above-trend core banking attrition" and core bank accounts down 2% YoY suggest switching costs are weaker than the narrative implies.
- **Competitors:** Fintechs — Adyen, Block, PayPal, Global Payments; tech giants expanding into payments — Apple, Google, Amazon; "modern core banking challengers"; peer benchmarks list ACI Worldwide (ACIW) and Amdocs (DOX).

## Financials
- **Growth rates:**
  - Revenue CAGR (5y): **7.37%** (peer table); forward Revenue CAGR (5y) only **1.50%**.
  - Net Income CAGR (5y): **29.4%**; forward Net Income CAGR (5y) **8.12%**.
  - Reported revenue rose 17,737→21,193 (2022→2025), then LTM slipped to 21,090 — growth has stalled and turned negative organically (**-3.6% YoY in Q1 26**).
  - EPS: diluted 3.91 (2022) → 6.34 (2025), LTM 5.91. Analyst EPS path: FY26 $8.08 (-6%), FY27 $8.60 (+10%), FY28 $9.32 (+12%). *Note the discrepancy between diluted EPS (~5.91) and adjusted EPS guidance ($8.00–$8.30) — adjusted figures exclude significant items.*
  - FCF CAGR: **Not explicitly given.** Operating cash flow: 4,618 (2022) → 6,013 (LTM); FCF yield now 14.9%, EV/FCF ~11.3x in 2025.
- **Returns on capital:** ROIC not stated directly. Proxies: **ROE 12.3%, ROI 7.56%, ROA 4.00%** — mediocre and below peers (ACIW ROE 13.8/ROI 11.0; DOX ROE 16.0/ROI 15.7). The low ROA reflects a $80.5B asset base (goodwill-heavy from acquisitions).
- **Balance sheet:** **Weak/stretched.** Total debt $29.3B (LTM) vs. only ~$829M cash; ~$27.9B long-term debt with $347M quarterly interest expense. Total equity $26.2B; Price/Book ~1.05. Management cites debt/EBITDA below 3.2x targeting ~3x by year-end — manageable but leaves little margin for error if FCF recovery stalls. Buybacks continue (3.3M shares/~$200M in Q1 26) despite the debt load.

## Risks
- **Regulatory:** Meaningful. Durbin Amendment debit-interchange dynamics are central — ironically, the STAR sale interest exists *because* banks want to bypass Durbin fee caps, which invites "backlash from lawmakers, regulators, and merchants." Rising cybersecurity compliance costs also flagged.
- **AI / tech disruption:** Double-edged. Threat from fintechs and tech giants entering payments; "modern core banking challengers" pressuring Financial Solutions. Fiserv is responding with Finxact (accounts +70% YoY), Project Elevate (AI efficiency), FIUSD stablecoin pilot, and agentic AI — but these are unproven and targets are deferred to Investor Day.
- **Customer/supplier concentration:** Not directly quantified. The STAR sale counterparties (JPMorgan, BofA, Wells, PNC) are also large banking clients, hinting at customer concentration in Financial Solutions, but the report does not give figures. **Largely not covered.**
- **Other red flags:**
  - **23 of 35 analysts cut FY26 EPS estimates in 90 days**; FY26 consensus fell 16.1% ($10.20→$8.56) over 12 months.
  - **Margin collapse:** adjusted operating margin 29.7% vs 37.8% YoY; Financial Solutions margin 47.5%→38.1%; Merchant 34%→26.4%.
  - **Leadership instability:** two CEOs in ~one year (Lyons out after ~12 months, Georgakopoulos in June 26).
  - **Back-half-weighted guidance:** full-year requires H2 margin of 35–36% after H1 at 31–32% — heavy execution risk.
  - Q1 EPS beat was partly a one-time 11% tax rate ($0.17 benefit), not operational.
  - Stock down ~67% in a year; -70.1% one-year total return.

## Valuation
- **Fair value / upside:** Report's fair value **$84.45** vs price ~$51.78 → **+57% upside**. Analyst targets range $40–$115, median/avg $70 (~+26.5% analyst upside). Recent ratings skew Hold, with a fresh **Sell from BNP Paribas Exane ($46)**.
- **Cheap, fair, or rich:** On the multiple, it screens **cheap** — forward P/E 6.4x (FY26) / 6.0x (FY27) vs 38x in FY24, EV/EBITDA 6.3x, FCF yield ~14.9%. But cheap is doing a lot of work: this is a classic "value trap vs. turnaround" setup. The multiple is low precisely because organic revenue is negative, margins are compressing, leverage is high, and estimates are still falling. It is cheap *if* the FY27 normalization thesis holds; fairly priced or worse if it doesn't.
- **Bull (what market may be missing):** Clover GPV +9% and VaaS +18% (27% of Clover revenue, high margin); Finxact +70%; Commerce Hub transactions +~200%; improving client service metrics (inquiry resolution -27%, high-impact incidents -60%) suggesting attrition may bend; STAR sale could delever/sharpen focus; insider buying and buybacks; ~90% FCF conversion.
- **Bear (what could go wrong):** Organic decline deepening (Financial Solutions -6% in Q1, Q2 expected worse), non-recurring revenue roll-off, structural core-banking attrition, CEO churn, high debt with thin cash, and a STAR deal that "several banks have concluded they are unlikely to pursue." H2 hockey-stick may not materialize.

## Verdict
Yes — a patient value investor should **dig deeper**, but with eyes open that this is a broken-momentum turnaround, not a clean compounder at a discount. The setup is genuinely interesting: a wide-moat processing franchise at 6x forward earnings and ~15% FCF yield, with real high-growth assets (Clover, Finxact) buried inside a declining legacy mix. But the price reflects legitimate deterioration — negative organic growth, 800bps of margin compression, $29B of debt against $0.8B cash, and two CEOs in a year — not just pessimism. The single most important thing to verify next is **whether core-banking attrition is actually stabilizing and the H2 organic re-acceleration is real** — specifically, watch Q2 (July 29) organic revenue and the exit-rate margin trajectory. The entire thesis rests on FY27 normalization; if Q2 confirms the trough and attrition bends, the discount is a gift, and if not, the "cheap" multiple simply follows earnings lower. Also independently confirm true owner-earnings (reconcile ~$5.91 GAAP diluted EPS against the $8.00–$8.30 adjusted number) and the STAR sale's real probability, since much of the bull catalyst leans on a deal banks are reportedly walking away from.
