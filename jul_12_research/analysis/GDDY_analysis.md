## Business
- **How does it earn money?** GoDaddy is the world's largest domain registrar (80M+ domains, 20M+ customers, 200+ markets). Revenue comes from a subscription-based recurring model split between the **Core Platform** segment (domains, hosting — ~60% of revenue, growing only ~3%) and the higher-margin **Applications & Commerce (A&C)** segment (~40% of revenue, growing 12% YoY at 45% EBITDA margins). ARR is $4.3B with >85% retention and ARPU of $246 (up 9%). The pivot story is toward AI products (Airo AI Builder, Airo for WordPress, Airo Care) and Agent Name Service (ANS).
- **Moat?** Yes, moderate. The primary moat is **brand + scale/cost advantage**: as the largest registrar, domains act as a massive low-cost customer-acquisition funnel that feeds higher-margin attach products (**switching costs** via multi-product adoption and >85% retention). A speculative new moat is a **data/network angle** — ANS positioning domains as the identity layer for the "agentic internet" via DNS. Owner-skeptic note: the core domain business is commoditized (rising Verisign fees are simply passed through) and growing only ~2.5%, so the durable moat is thinner than the "largest registrar" framing suggests.
- **Competitors:** Shopify, Wix, Squarespace, Hostinger, and Cloudflare in web presence/hosting; peer benchmarks in the report cite PayPal and Yelp. Large cloud providers entering the SMB space are a rising AI threat.

## Financials
- **Growth rates:**
  - Revenue: 5-yr CAGR **8.34%**; LTM revenue $5.02B, growing ~6–8%. Forecast 5-yr revenue CAGR only **5.22%** — decelerating.
  - EPS: Analyst-forecast growth of 17.0% (FY26), 14.8% (FY27), 4.1% (FY28). GAAP diluted EPS is noisy — $9.08 (2023, tax-benefit inflated), $6.45 (2024), $6.22 (2025), $6.31 LTM — so reported "EPS CAGR" is distorted; much of per-share growth comes from buybacks (shares down >31% since 2022 to 133M), not net income (which fell from $1,374M in 2023 to $870M LTM). Net income 5-yr CAGR **4.49%**; forecast net income CAGR **11.9%**.
  - FCF: TTM FCF $1.68B, targeting ~$1.8B FY26; company cites a "20% FCF CAGR North Star" it expects to exceed. Q1 26 FCF +15% to $474M.
- **ROIC / returns on capital:** ROI **25.0%**, ROA **10.9%**. ROE of **398%** is meaningless — it reflects near-zero/negative equity from buyback-driven balance sheet, not genuine capital efficiency. The 25% ROI is the more useful signal and is respectable.
- **Debt / balance sheet:** Total debt ~$3.85B against total equity of just $237M (equity was *negative* in 2022). $1.3B cash, $2.3B total liquidity. **Short-term obligations exceed liquid assets** (current liabilities $3.06B vs current assets $2.05B) — flagged as a financial-health concern. This is a leveraged, buyback-financed balance sheet: FCF is strong and covers it, but there is little equity cushion and Price/Book of ~49x is a symptom of that leverage, not strength.

## Risks
- **Regulatory / legal:** (1) **Securities law investigation** by Kaplan Fox & Kilsheimer LLP into whether disclosures on promotional domain pricing complied with securities laws — the central overhang. (2) **FTC order (finalized May 25)** requiring a comprehensive information-security program after alleged 2019–2022 data-security failures — ongoing compliance/audit costs. (3) Revised Universal Terms of Service (Feb 26) reclassifying all users as "business customers" — potential churn/dispute risk.
- **AI / technological disruption:** Double-edged. GoDaddy is investing heavily (Airo, ANS), but AI is **immaterial to near-term financials** and execution is early-stage. Risk that well-capitalized rivals (large cloud providers) **commoditize AI SMB tools before GoDaddy scales**. Management also admitted some search-traffic loss to "AI mode" (customer journeys starting in AI interfaces) — a structural threat to the domain funnel.
- **Customer/supplier concentration:** No customer concentration (20M+ SMBs). Supplier side: dependence on **Verisign** (registry fee rising $10.26→$10.97 Nov 26) and a **.co registry contract expiration** that hurt bookings.
- **Other red flags:** **Heavy one-sided insider selling** — 22 sales, 0 purchases over six months, including the CFO — signaling weak internal conviction at these prices. **Bookings decelerated to 3%** (core platform bookings *declined* 1%), consistently lagging revenue, driven by the one-year promotional domain strategy. Stock down ~58% from its high. Options activity dominated by puts. No dividend.

## Valuation
- **Fair value:** Report's technical/DCF fair value **$128.76** (~45.5% upside vs $88.51); InvestingPro's separate estimate is **$122.93** (~61.5% upside cited elsewhere). Analyst targets span $83 (Wells Fargo) to $190 (Benchmark), median ~$112, but the report notes consensus has drifted down to **~$77** on recent news — a wide, deteriorating spread signaling genuine uncertainty.
- **Cheap, fair, or rich?** On the surface **cheap**: forward P/E ~9.45x, PEG 0.63, EV/EBITDA ~8x, FCF yield ~14%. For a company still growing revenue ~6% and buying back >31% of shares, these are demanding-of-attention low multiples. The caveat: the multiple compressed *for reasons* (bookings deceleration, legal probe, insider selling), so the market is pricing a real risk of stalled growth, not just pessimism.
- **What the market may be missing (bull):** A&C is now 40% of revenue at 45% EBITDA margins and growing double digits — the mix shift alone should lift consolidated margins; Airo attach (~30% faster second-product attachment, ARPU +9%) could re-accelerate ARPU; buybacks compound per-share value at a 14% FCF yield; ANS optionality is free. If bookings recover in H2 26 as promised, the stock is materially undervalued.
- **What could go wrong (bear):** Core domain business is maturing (~2.5% growth) and the promotional strategy may be masking structural demand weakness, not timing. AI could erode the domain funnel faster than Airo monetizes. The securities probe could produce a genuine disclosure problem. Insider selling suggests those with the best information don't see the bull case. If growth settles at ~5% and AI stays immaterial, the "cheap" multiple is a value trap.

## Verdict
Yes — a patient value investor should dig deeper, because the setup is a legitimately cash-generative, market-leading franchise (14% FCF yield, 25% ROI, aggressive buybacks, high-margin A&C mix shift) trading at ~9x forward earnings after a 58% drawdown, which is exactly where value opportunities live. But the skepticism must be genuine: the low multiple coincides with a securities investigation, one-sided insider selling, and core bookings actually *declining*. The single most important thing to verify next is **whether the bookings deceleration is truly a timing artifact of the one-year promotional-domain strategy (as management claims, with recovery expected in H2 26) or a structural demand plateau in the core domain business** — because that one question determines whether the 14% FCF yield is a gift or a value trap. Confirming it requires the Q2 26 print (Aug 5, 2026) showing bookings re-converging toward revenue growth, plus clarity on the Kaplan Fox investigation's substance.
