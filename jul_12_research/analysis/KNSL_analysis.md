## Business

**How does it earn money?** Kinsale is a U.S. specialty insurer operating exclusively in the Excess & Surplus (E&S) lines market — underwriting "hard-to-place" risks that standard carriers decline (bars, contractors, demolition firms, cannabis dispensaries, jet-ski rentals) across all 50 states. It earns an underwriting profit on premiums (combined ratio 77.4% in Q1 26, meaning ~22.6 cents of underwriting profit per premium dollar) plus investment income on its float. Net investment income grew 26.5% YoY with float at $3.3B and new-money yields ~5% — a growing second earnings engine.

**Moat?** The report frames a genuine, if modest, moat built on two reinforcing advantages:
- **Cost advantage** — a proprietary technology platform with *no legacy software*, driving a low other-underwriting-expense ratio (10.3% in Q1 26) and faster broker service. Management argues this lets it adopt AI faster than encumbered incumbents.
- **Underwriting/data skill** — a 17-year track record of favorable reserve development and consistent 20%+ ROE (26.4% FY25). This is a "sharp-pencil" cultural moat rather than a structural one; it is not switching costs or network effects. Notably, the Bear Cave-cited **60% retention rate vs. ~90% industry norm** actively argues *against* customer-level switching costs.

**Competitors:** Peers benchmarked are **Arch Capital (ACGL)** and **Chubb (CB)**. The report also flags competitive pressure from **MGAs, fronting companies, and admitted carriers re-entering E&S** — particularly in large commercial property and long-tail casualty/construction.

## Financials

- **Revenue CAGR (5y):** 32.4% (vs. ACGL 18.6%, CB 10.3%) — best-in-class historically. LTM revenue $1.9B. Note the forward outlook decelerates hard: **Revenue Forecast CAGR (5y) just 3.23%**.
- **EPS growth:** Diluted EPS grew 37.7% YoY in Q1 26; but analyst forward EPS growth is modest — 6.7% (2026), 4.2% (2027), 5.6% (2028). **Net Income CAGR (5y): 41.6%** historical vs. **1.22% forecast** — a stark deceleration.
- **FCF CAGR:** Not cleanly given (Levered FCF rows show 0.0 in annual tables). FCF Yield ~12.4% current; historical FCF Yield ran 8.9%–10.6% (2023–25). Cash from operations LTM $1.06B.
- **ROIC / returns on capital:** ROE 29.7% (LTM), ROI 27.2%, ROA 9.22% — all above both peers. Annualized ROE 24% in Q1 26, 26.4% FY25. Management targets low-20s% ROE. These are excellent, well above cost of capital.
- **Balance sheet:** Conservatively levered — Total Debt just $224.5M against $1.97B equity (Debt/Total Capital ~10%). Interest well covered. **Red flag:** short-term obligations exceed liquid assets (Total Current Liabilities $3.99B vs. Current Assets $795M) — though for an insurer, current liabilities are dominated by loss reserves and unearned premium, so this is less alarming than for an industrial, but the report flags it as a genuine liquidity note.

## Risks

- **Regulatory:** Elevated. Bear Cave's overcharging allegations already prompted at least one state regulator complaint (Colorado, Raven Security Group — $25,427 policy excluding professional liability, assault & battery, and firearms for an *armed security* company). Broader regulatory change could erode E&S's rate/form freedom advantage — the core of Kinsale's business model.
- **AI / technological disruption:** Framed as an *opportunity* more than a threat — no legacy software, AI agents being embedded into underwriting/claims. But the flip side is that Kinsale's edge is partly a tech-and-data edge that better-capitalized rivals could close.
- **Customer/supplier concentration:** No single-customer concentration flagged. The real concentration is **business-line and market concentration** — 100% E&S, no admitted or international diversification, so results track the E&S cycle. Within that, large commercial property is the acute pain point.
- **Other red flags:**
  - **60% retention rate** vs. ~90% norm — questions the durability of the book and whether pricing is punitive.
  - **GWP turned negative (-0.5%) in Q1 26**; Amwins Pricing Index -3.3% (worsening from -2.7%). Large commercial property avg. premium fell from >$50k to $30–40k/policy; **E&S Homeowners -22%**.
  - Social inflation / claims severity in long-tail lines could outpace pricing.
  - Catastrophe exposure (mitigated by reinsurance but structural).
  - A Form 144 (potential insider sale) filed May 26.

## Valuation

- **Fair value:** $506.1, implying **+42.6% upside** from $354.9 (an earlier Morgan Stanley-cited InvestingPro FV was $469.67). P/E ~15.5x LTM, Fwd P/E ~17.4x, **PEG 0.51**, P/B 4.16x, EV/EBITDA 4.62.
- **Cheap, fair, or rich?** Mixed. On earnings/growth (PEG 0.51, forward P/E 17x against a 24%+ ROE compounder) it screens *cheap*. On **Price/Book 4.16x** — the more honest yardstick for an insurer — it is *not cheap*; the report explicitly notes "high Price/Book multiple, limiting margin of safety if earnings growth disappoints." So the whole thesis hinges on whether ROE stays in the mid-20s. At that ROE, 4x book is defensible; if ROE fades toward low-20s and growth stalls, the multiple compresses.
- **What the market may be missing (bull):** E&S market ~$130–145B and growing (+223% over the decade), Kinsale holds <2% share — long organic runway; margins in small-to-mid accounts remain robust (submissions +6%, quotes +8%, binds +9%); net written premium still grew 5.6% despite GWP -0.5%; insider buying near the low; 8 up vs. 3 down EPS revisions.
- **What could go wrong (bear):** The current softening cycle deepens; the deceleration from 40%+ to low-single-digit forecast growth becomes permanent; the 60% retention signals a churning, price-gouged book that unwinds when the cycle turns; regulatory action on pricing/exclusions; and a 4x book multiple with any of the above.

## Verdict

Yes — a patient, owner-minded investor should want to dig deeper. This is a demonstrably high-quality underwriter: 26%+ ROE, a 17-year clean-reserve record, a real low-cost/tech edge, a fortress-conservative balance sheet, and a long E&S runway at <2% share — trading down ~37% with insiders buying. That is exactly the "great business, temporarily out of favor" setup value investors hunt for. But the price (4x book) already pays for continued excellence, so there is little margin of safety, and two things must be true for the thesis to hold: durable underwriting quality and a cyclical (not structural) growth slowdown. **The single most important thing to verify next is the 60% retention rate** — is it a genuine ~90%-norm-shattering red flag about an over-priced, churning book (as Bear Cave alleges), or an artifact of Kinsale's small-account, non-renewing E&S mix? That number is the fulcrum between "best-in-class compounder on sale" and "value trap whose reserves and pricing are riskier than the combined ratio suggests." Confirm it against actual renewal hit-ratio disclosure and reserve-development trends before committing capital.
