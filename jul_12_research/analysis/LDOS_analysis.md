## Business
- **How it earns money:** Leidos is a U.S. technology/engineering/science services and integration firm, earning ~87% of FY'25 revenue from U.S. government contracts (defense, intelligence, civil, health). LTM revenue $17.3B, split across National Security Solutions (~$9.9B), Civil (~$5.1B), Defense Solutions (~$2.2B), and Health (~$1.2B). Recently added defense *product* manufacturing (cruise missiles, hypersonics) alongside its traditional services base.
- **Moat:** The report argues for durable advantages from privileged access to federal digital infrastructure, security clearances, proprietary data, incumbency, and scale/prime-contractor capability that smaller rivals can't credibly match. Backlog of $48.4B (1.1x TTM book-to-bill) reflects deep, sticky customer relationships. Realistically this is a moderate moat — high switching costs and regulatory/clearance barriers — but it is a services business exposed to recompetes and pricing pressure, not a fortress.
- **Competitors:** Booz Allen Hamilton, SAIC, CACI, General Dynamics; Oracle Health specifically for the EHR integration role.

## Financials
- **Growth:** Revenue CAGR (5y) 6.91%; forecast revenue CAGR (5y) 3.96% (decelerating). Net income CAGR (5y) 18.2%; forecast net-income CAGR (5y) only 4.93%. EPS forecast growth is thin: FY'26 +2.9% (to $12.55), FY'27 +6.5%, FY'28 +10.5%. FCF CAGR: Not explicitly given; CFO grew from $992M (2022) to $1,993M LTM, Q1'26 FCF $270M, FY'26 OCF guide ~$1.8B.
- **Returns on capital:** ROE 30.5% (SWOT cites 28.4%), ROI 13.8%, ROA 10.0% — genuinely strong versus peers (J at 11.5% ROE, FCN at 14.0%). True ROIC not separately stated.
- **Balance sheet:** Total debt $6.9B LTM (report cites $6.3B/2.6x gross leverage post-ENTRUST), against equity $5.1B and only $457M cash. Interest coverage historically healthy but debt rose meaningfully to fund ENTRUST. Cash Flow Rating just 5/10 — flagged as constraining flexibility. Manageable but no longer conservative.

## Risks
- **Regulatory/government exposure:** The dominant risk — ~87% of revenue from U.S. government; exposed to budget fluctuations, continuing resolutions, sequestration, shutdowns, procurement delays.
- **AI/tech disruption:** Report frames AI as an accelerant, not a threat, citing data/clearance moats. Skeptically, this is unproven — commoditization of integration work is a real long-term risk not deeply examined.
- **Customer concentration:** Extreme single-customer (U.S. government) concentration. Health segment concentrated in DHA EHR (transitioning away to Oracle Health, phased Jul'26 into '27) and VBA MDE contract facing recompete plus a new fourth vendor.
- **Other red flags:** EPS revisions 11 down vs 1 up (90d); two downgrades (BofA to $125, Jefferies to Hold, FY'27 EPS cut to $12.95); stock down ~37% from high; Strong Sell technicals; Q2'26 flagged as trough; margin compression in Defense (8.3% from 9.8%) and Homeland (8.5% from 9.4%) on fixed-price program issues; Q1 beat partly a pull-forward from Q2; CapEx running behind ($31M vs $87M/qtr run-rate), signaling slower production ramp.

## Valuation
- **Fair value:** Pro fair value $156.23 → +43.5% upside from $108.8. Median analyst target $168.27 (+54.4% upside); range $110–$225.
- **Cheap/fair/rich:** Screens genuinely cheap on the numbers — forward P/E ~8.67x (FY'26) / 8.26x (FY'27), EV/EBITDA 8.1x, FCF yield 13.6%, PEG 0.94, P/B 2.73, vs peer J at 36.8x P/E. Trading near 52-week low after a ~33% one-year decline. Multiple compressed from 16x to under 9x in a year.
- **Bull (what market may be missing):** Record $48.4B backlog, defense-tech momentum ($15.6B LCCM framework, $2.7B hypersonics, $24B cyber pipeline), aggressive buybacks ($200M in Q1), 7-year dividend growth, and strong FCF yield — if execution stabilizes, re-rating is large.
- **Bear (what could go wrong):** Health segment structurally impaired (DHA loss, VBA recompete), anemic organic growth, self-funded LCCM munitions bet with uncertain returns, elevated leverage, negative estimate momentum. The low multiple may be the market correctly pricing a services franchise losing a marquee program with no near-term growth catalyst.

## Verdict
Yes — a patient value investor should dig deeper. The setup is classic value: a cash-generative, high-ROE government-services incumbent trading at ~8-9x earnings and a 13.6% FCF yield after a 33%+ drawdown, with a fortress backlog and real defense tailwinds, priced as if the healthcare deterioration is terminal. The tension is whether the low multiple is opportunity or an accurate read on structural decline. The single most important thing to verify next is the true magnitude and margin impact of the Health segment loss — quantify the DHA EHR revenue/profit at risk and the probability/size of the VBA MDE recompete loss — because that determines whether "cheap" reflects a temporary air-pocket or permanent earnings impairment. Secondarily, confirm how much of FY'26 EPS is genuine organic growth versus ENTRUST accretion and Q2 pull-forward.
