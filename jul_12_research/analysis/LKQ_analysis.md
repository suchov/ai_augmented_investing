## Business
- **How it earns money:** LKQ is a global distributor of alternative and specialty auto parts — OEM recycled, aftermarket, and remanufactured components. LTM revenue is $13.8B split across Europe (~$6.4B), NA Wholesale (~$2.9B), Specialty (~$1.7B), and Self-Service (~$129M). It sells to collision repair shops, insurers, and multi-shop operators (MSOs), monetizing the gap between OEM-new parts and cheaper alternatives.
- **Moat:** The report implies a moat from **scale and distribution density** (dominant global network, "leading distributor") plus **switching costs / integration lock-in** — deepening MSO integrations that automate ordering and structurally raise alternative-parts adoption (record APU ~40%), and entrenched insurer relationships driving recurring volume. This is a cost-advantage/distribution moat, not a brand or network-effects one. Notably, gross margin is a healthy 38.3%, but operating margin is thin (~8%), suggesting the moat is real in sourcing but competed away in distribution.
- **Competitors:** Peers cited are Lear (LEA) and Gentex (GNTX), though these aren't direct distribution comps. The report names the real threat as "local, national, and online automotive parts providers" plus OEM-new parts, and competitive pricing especially in Europe (U.K., Italy).

## Financials
- **Growth rates:** Revenue CAGR (5y) **3.26%** (vs. LEA 6.41%, GNTX 8.46%); forecast revenue CAGR (5y) 2.79%. **Net income CAGR (5y) is -0.99%** — essentially flat-to-declining. EPS trend is clearly deteriorating: diluted EPS fell from $4.13 (2022) → $3.49 (2023) → $2.62 (2024) → $2.35 (2025) → $2.01 LTM. FCF CAGR: not cleanly given (Levered FCF rows show 0.0), but Cash from Operations fell from $1,356M (2023) to $1,010M LTM.
- **ROIC / returns on capital:** No explicit ROIC. ROE 8.15%, ROI 6.68%, ROA 3.38% — all mediocre and below peers. Profitability rating 5.8/10. These returns are unimpressive for a "dominant" business.
- **Balance sheet:** Total debt $5.2B against $6.5B equity; leverage ratio **2.6x EBITDA**, effective interest rate 5.0%, described as investment-grade. Liquid assets exceed short-term obligations. **Red flag:** a $500M term loan came current at Q1-end requiring refinancing; Q1 FCF was **-$96M** (worse than -$57M prior year). Book value/share $25.3 sits roughly at the $26.6 price (P/B ~1.05).

## Risks
- **Regulatory / trade:** Section 232 tariffs are the primary margin drag in NA wholesale — cited as "nearly the entire" YoY NA EBITDA margin decline. Management expects comps to normalize in H2 '26 as tariffs anniversary, but this is a genuine ongoing exposure.
- **AI / technological disruption:** The structural risk here is **EVs, not AI** — EVs require ~60% less parts/maintenance value than ICE vehicles, an existential threat to the core repairable-parts model. Partial offset: rising collision-repair calibration needs (~75% of repairs vs. 62% three years ago) via Elitek, and small EV-battery/electronics remanufacturing tuck-ins.
- **Customer / supplier concentration:** Not covered directly, though heavy reliance on insurers and MSOs concentrates demand drivers (claim rates, insurance premiums, total-loss rates).
- **Other red flags:** 8 downward EPS revisions in 90 days and zero upward; a $0.17/share Mekonomen impairment; Europe persistently weak (EBITDA 7.8%, -150bps); Specialty sale delayed by tightening credit markets; no buybacks in Q1.

## Valuation
- **Fair value / upside:** Report fair value **$35.43 vs. $26.6 price → +33.4% upside**. Analyst avg target $40.81 (range $33–$47.50) implies larger upside. Forward P/E 8.89x (FY26), 8.09x (FY27); EV/EBITDA 7.81x; FCF yield 11.9%; div yield 4.52%.
- **Cheap/fair/rich:** On multiples it looks **cheap** — sub-9x forward earnings, double-digit FCF yield, P/B ~1.0. But cheapness reflects declining earnings (EPS down four straight years) and a negative PEG (-0.52). This is a classic value trap candidate unless the NA recovery (organic decline improving to -0.5%) is real.
- **Bull (what market may miss):** Strategic review with BofA/Goldman could unlock value — takeover chatter in the "low-to-mid $50s." NA organic trend inflecting, record APU, Specialty back to growth (+3.4%), used-car values rising (+6.2% in March) lifting repairable claims, European private-label pricing gains landing in '27. Reaffirmed FY26 EPS $2.90–$3.20 and FCF $700M–$850M would put FCF yield near 12%.
- **Bear (what could go wrong):** EV secular decline, Europe not recovering, refinancing/leverage strain, delayed/failed Specialty sale, and margins that keep compressing despite revenue "growth" that is largely FX/commodity-driven, not organic.

## Verdict
Yes — a patient value investor should dig deeper, but with clear eyes about whether this is undervaluation or terminal decline. The setup is attractive on paper (8.9x earnings, ~12% FCF yield, 4.5% yield, 2.6x leverage, a live strategic review with credible bankers), but the fundamentals are moving the wrong way: EPS has fallen four consecutive years, returns on capital are pedestrian (ROE ~8%, ROIC not disclosed), and the EV transition is a genuine long-term erosion of the core. The single most important thing to verify next is **the durability and cash conversion of free cash flow** — specifically whether management's $700M–$850M FY26 FCF guide is achievable after a -$96M Q1, because that number underpins both the dividend and the ability to refinance the $500M term loan without dilution or distress. Secondarily, confirm whether the NA organic inflection (-0.5%) is a real trend or a one-quarter tariff/FX artifact.
