## Business
- **How it earns money:** Newmont is the world's largest gold producer, selling gold doré ($4.8B/qtr) and concentrate ($2.5B/qtr) from a diversified mine portfolio across the Americas, Africa, Australia, and Papua New Guinea. It has meaningful by-product revenue from copper (~15% of revenue, targeting 150,000 tonnes/yr) and silver. LTM revenue $25.0B, net income $8.5B.
- **Moat:** Mining is fundamentally a commodity, price-taker business — there is no pricing power. The closest thing to a moat is a **cost/asset advantage**: a base of Tier 1, long-life assets with ~118.2M oz of proven & probable reserves (production visibility), geographic diversification, and scale as the only gold miner in the S&P 500. Note Q1 AISC of $1,029/oz was well below peers, but full-year AISC is guided sharply higher to ~$1,680/oz, which undercuts any durable cost-moat claim. This is a decent asset base, not a true economic moat.
- **Main competitors:** Barrick Mining ("B", $61.8B mkt cap) and Kinross Gold ("KGC", $28.5B) are the named peers; the report also references Agnico Eagle, Gold Fields, AngloGold Ashanti, Harmony, Sibanye, Coeur, Hecla. Barrick is also a JV partner in Nevada Gold Mines (currently in dispute).

## Financials
- **Revenue CAGR:** 5-year 14.5% (vs. 6.1% Barrick, 10.8% Kinross). Notably, the report shows a **5-year forward revenue CAGR of –9.84%**, implying the Street models declining top-line ahead.
- **EPS CAGR:** Not given directly; trajectory is volatile — diluted EPS swung from –$2.97 (2023) to $2.92 (2024) to $6.39 (2025) to $7.69 LTM. Analysts project +47% FY26, +10.8% FY27, +5.8% FY28. Net income 5y CAGR 20.2%.
- **FCF CAGR:** Not stated as a CAGR. Absolute FCF: FY25 $7.3B, Q1'26 record $3.1B. FCF yield 9.18% (LTM); the report's LFCF line reads $0 for most historical periods (data gap) so a clean series isn't provided.
- **ROIC / returns on capital:** No true ROIC given. Proxies: ROE 25.6% (22% LTM per SWOT), ROI 22.2%, ROA 15.2%. Respectable but note these are boosted by peak gold prices.
- **Debt / balance sheet:** Healthy and improving. Total debt fell from $9.5B (2023) to $5.5B (LTM) against $35.1B equity — low leverage. Current assets $13.5B cover current liabilities $5.5B (~2.4x). Interest is well covered. Piotroski Score of 9 (perfect). Described as "moderate debt."

## Risks
- **Regulatory:** Elevated. Ghana introduced a sliding-scale royalty (~$25/oz headwind) and is demanding contractor localization by end-2026; potential tax hikes in Latin America and Papua New Guinea; resource nationalism broadly.
- **AI / tech disruption:** Not covered in report (low relevance for a gold miner; no disruption thesis discussed).
- **Customer/supplier concentration:** Customer concentration not covered (gold is sold into a liquid global market). Supplier/operational concentration risk noted via heavy **reliance on contractors** for major activities, raising cost/safety/productivity risk.
- **Other red flags:**
  - **Commodity price is the dominant risk** — gold fell to ~7-month lows (~$4,031–4,074/oz) on a hawkish Fed and stronger dollar; revenue/FCF are highly gold-price sensitive.
  - **Simultaneous C-suite overhaul** (new CFO, COO, CTO, Chief Accounting Officer) effective July 1, 2026 — significant execution/continuity risk during volatility.
  - **Margin compression:** AISC rising to ~$1,680/oz from $1,358; 2026 is a planned production trough; energy inflation ($12/oz per $10/bbl Brent).
  - **Nevada Gold Mines JV dispute** — open-ended notice of default vs. Barrick (Feb 2026), possible arbitration/litigation.
  - **Negative estimate momentum:** 3 downward EPS revisions in 90 days, 5 for the upcoming period; technicals flash **Strong Sell**.
  - Cadia earthquake (April 2026) temporarily disrupted operations.

## Valuation
- **Fair value & upside:** Report's fair value $125.92 vs. price $95.1 → **+32.5% upside**. Analyst median target $138.14 (~+45%), range $67–$175. Forward P/E 9.62; EV/EBITDA 5.7; PEG 0.16.
- **Cheap, fair, or rich?** On the surface **cheap** — single-digit forward P/E, 9%+ FCF yield, low PEG. But the skeptic must adjust: these multiples sit on **peak-cycle gold prices (~$4,000/oz)** and peak earnings. The forward revenue CAGR of –9.84% and rising AISC suggest the "E" may be near a top. Low P/Es on cyclicals at peak earnings are a classic value trap. So it's cheap *if* gold holds; otherwise fairer than it looks.
- **What the market may be missing (bull):** Production recovery to ~6M oz in 2027 (Ahafo North, Tanami Ex 2, Cadia panel caves, Lihir/Boddington high grades), aggressive buybacks (two $6B programs; per-share FCF already +6%), 56-year dividend record, and undervaluation vs. record cash generation.
- **What could go wrong (bear):** Gold reverts (Macquarie already cut its outlook to $4,300–4,450), AISC overshoots guidance, the new management team stumbles, Ghana/JV disputes escalate, and 2026 trough earnings disappoint — with negative revision momentum already underway.

## Verdict
A patient value investor could justify a closer look — NEM offers a world-class, long-reserve asset base, a genuinely strong balance sheet (net debt low, Piotroski 9), record FCF, disciplined shareholder returns, and an optically cheap multiple with a stated 32.5% margin of safety. But the buffett/lynch discipline flags a caution: this is a **price-taking commodity producer with no true moat**, and its cheap multiples rest on peak gold prices and peak earnings while AISC is climbing and the forward revenue CAGR is negative. The single most important thing to verify next is **the normalized/mid-cycle earnings and free cash flow at a conservative gold price (well below the current ~$4,000/oz) net of the rising ~$1,680/oz AISC** — i.e., is the valuation still attractive if you don't assume today's gold price persists? Secondarily, confirm the 2027 production ramp is on track and that the new C-suite can execute the trough-to-recovery transition.
