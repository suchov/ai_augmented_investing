## Business
- **How it earns money:** Pool Corp is the world's largest wholesale distributor of swimming pool supplies, equipment, and outdoor-living products. It operates ~455 sales centers, carries 200,000+ SKUs, and serves ~125,000 customers (builders, remodelers, service companies). LTM revenue $5,355M at ~29.7% gross margin — a classic high-volume, thin-gross-margin distribution model. Critically, ~62% of sales are non-discretionary maintenance products (chemicals, replacement parts), an annuity-like recurring base, while new-pool construction and large remodels are the discretionary swing factor.
- **Moat:** Yes, primarily a **scale/cost advantage** — ~40–50% market share, the densest sales-center network, and 200k SKUs create high barriers vs. fragmented regional/local supply houses. Secondary moats: the **Pool360 digital platform** (13% of Q1 sales, some branches >30%, target >25%) which lowers cost-to-serve and raises switching costs, and growing **proprietary/private-label brands** (REGAL, EZ Clor, Xtreme Tabs) that lift margins. The 5.5M aging in-ground installed base gives durable replacement demand independent of new builds.
- **Competitors:** Regional distributors, local supply houses, and e-commerce platforms. Peer benchmarks cite Applied Industrial (AIT) and Watsco (WSO.B), though those are adjacent distributors rather than direct pool competitors.

## Financials
- **Growth:** Revenue CAGR (5y) 6.09% per peer table; but recent trend is stagnation — revenue fell from a 2022 peak of $6,179M to $5,289M in 2025 (LTM $5,355M). Net income CAGR (5y) only 2.08%. EPS has declined sharply from $18.7 (2022) to $10.9 LTM as post-COVID demand normalized. Forward analyst EPS growth is modest: 3.3% (2026), 8.7% (2027), 8.3% (2028). FCF: report shows EV/FCF 19.0→25.4 (2023–25) and FCF yield ~3.9%; note the "Levered FCF" line reads 0.0, and cash from operations collapsed from $888M (2023) to $364M LTM — worth scrutinizing.
- **Returns on capital:** Strong. ROE 34.1%, ROI 17.0%, ROA 10.5%. Profitability rating 8/10. These are attractive returns for a distributor and the core of the quality case.
- **Balance sheet:** Moderate leverage. Total debt $1,591M LTM (text repeatedly says "~$1.2B"; there is a discrepancy between the narrative and the statements worth verifying), leverage ratio 1.7x, debt/equity ~129.5. Equity has eroded ($1,235M in 2022 → $1,133M LTM) partly from buybacks. Liquid assets exceed short-term obligations; interest expense rising to $49–51M in '26 due to buyback-related borrowing.

## Risks
- **Regulatory:** Limited direct regulatory moat/threat, but trade policy/tariff cost inflation was cited as a '25 pricing factor and could pressure margins if not passed through. Equipment efficiency regulations (post-2018 variable-speed pump/LED rules) are actually a tailwind for replacement.
- **AI/tech disruption:** Not a major theme; the tech angle is offensive (Pool360). E-commerce platforms are cited as a competitive pressure on pricing/share.
- **Customer/supplier concentration:** Not covered in report (customer base of ~125,000 appears diversified; supplier concentration not disclosed).
- **Other red flags:** (1) EPS revisions heavily negative — 9 down vs. 1 up over 90 days despite the Q1 beat; (2) S&P 500 removal in June '26 (replaced by Marvell), a passive-flow/technical overhang; (3) depressed new-pool construction (~58,000 units, no recovery expected); (4) gross margin dipped 20 bps on unfavorable mix and early-buy discounts; (5) inventory up 14% YoY to $1.7B; (6) seasonal/weather sensitivity (Florida, California, Texas); (7) new CEO (Watwood) leadership transition; (8) PEG of 9.74 flags valuation-vs-growth disconnect.

## Valuation
- **Fair value:** InvestingPro pegs fair value at ~$275–$292 (multiple citations: $274.69, $275.38, $282.84, headline $291.5), implying ~33–49% upside from ~$195–$220. Analyst median target ~$255 (high $300, low $210), skewed to Hold (8 hold, 5 buy, 1 sell).
- **Cheap/fair/rich:** Mixed signals. On earnings power the stock looks reasonable — P/E 19.8 (fwd 19.7), EV/EBITDA 14.9, down from ~28–29x P/E in 2023–24, a genuine de-rating. But Price/Book ~7x and PEG 9.74 are rich relative to low-single-digit near-term growth. So: cheap vs. its own history and vs. quality metrics (34% ROE); not statistically cheap on book or growth-adjusted terms. The bull case rests on normalized/through-cycle earnings, not current growth.
- **Bull (what market may be missing):** Durable ~62% non-discretionary base + 5.5M aging installed pools + an accelerating post-2018 equipment replacement cycle mean the thesis doesn't need new-construction recovery. Pool360 and private-label margin accretion, 15-year dividend growth streak, $600M buyback authorization, and a $1.9M insider purchase at ~$190 all support a floor. S&P 500 removal may have created forced, non-fundamental selling.
- **Bear (what could go wrong):** Prolonged high mortgage rates keep housing turnover and discretionary pool spend depressed; margins pressured by mix, tariffs, and rising interest expense; negative EPS revision momentum; passive outflows post-index removal; and valuation on book/PEG leaves little margin of safety if estimates keep falling.

## Verdict
Yes — a patient value investor should dig deeper. This is a demonstrably high-quality, wide-moat distributor (34% ROE, dominant share, recurring maintenance base) trading down ~35–41% and materially below credible fair-value estimates, with insider buying and aggressive buybacks near the lows — the exact setup value investors hunt for. The catch is that the "cheapness" depends on normalized earnings, and near-term momentum (9-vs-1 negative revisions, flat-to-down revenue, index removal) is genuinely poor. **The single most important thing to verify next is free cash flow quality:** operating cash flow fell from $888M (2023) to ~$364M LTM while the "Levered FCF" line shows zero and inventory jumped 14% — I would reconcile true owner earnings/FCF conversion (and the $1.2B-vs-$1.6B debt discrepancy) before trusting the ~3.9% FCF yield and the fair-value case.
